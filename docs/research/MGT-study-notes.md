## THE STRUCTURAL FACT for Article 2: scope excludes rank-and-file firefighters

- Executive Summary, p. 2 (opening line): "MGT is pleased to have had the opportunity to work with the County of Maui on this Classification and Compensation Study **for their Directors and Deputy Directors.**"
- **Verified by keyword sweep of the whole 92-page report:** ZERO occurrences of Firefighter (as a class), Fire Captain, Battalion Chief, Fire Engineer, Ocean Safety, or Bargaining Unit. The ONLY fire positions in the study are **Fire Chief (Grade 14)** and **Deputy Fire Chief (Grade 13).**
- → **The county commissioned, and paid for, a market compensation study to keep its department *heads* competitive — and did not study the firefighters who work under them.** Firefighter (BU-11/HFFA) pay is set by arbitration instead (which the Chief said came in "lower than most contracts"). Same employer, two different treatments.

## THE COST-OF-LIVING / WILDFIRE LANGUAGE (verbatim, pp. 15–16)

> "When the Salary Commission determines a pay percentile, it is crucial to consider how the **cost of living in Maui County impacts both recruitment and retention**, and how this factor should influence the chosen pay percentile. Additionally, **the Directors and Deputy Directors in Maui County have faced numerous unique challenges in the aftermath of the tragic fires**, which should also be factored into the decision-making process.
>
> According to the most recent data from livingcost.org, **the cost of living in Maui County is 1.3 times higher than the U.S. average, and Hawaii ranks as the 4th most expensive state to live in.** This high cost of living creates a distinct challenge, **potentially deterring qualified candidates** from pursuing positions with Maui County and making it harder to retain current employees."

- → The county's own consultant used **cost of living** and **the fires** as reasons to pick a **higher pay percentile — explicitly for the Directors and Deputy Directors.** These are the exact same justifications the firefighter union makes for the rank-and-file, applied here only to leadership. (Note the study attributes the fire hardship to "Directors and Deputy Directors," not to the firefighters who fought the fire.)

## Percentile framework (pp. 15–16)
- MGT priced positions at the 50th, 60th, 65th, 75th, 80th percentiles of comparable jurisdictions.
- "50th percentile = fair market rate / middle"; "65th = above average, 15% higher than the middle of the market"; "75th = market leader."
- Two plan options; **Option 2 presented at 50th / 65th / 75th percentile.** Deputies set a fixed % below their Director (**Deputy Fire Chief 5% below the Fire Chief** per MGT; note the Fire Commission separately used a "90% of Chief" = 10%-below ratio — minor discrepancy to flag).

## Methodology (defensible; not a union document)
- Comparable jurisdictions: cities pop. 120,000–400,000 in Western Region states (AK, AZ, CA, CO, HI, ID, MT, NV, NM, OR, UT, WA, WY) + consolidated city-county entities + Hawaii/Honolulu/Kauai counties; 94-point similarity cutoff; final comparable set of agencies that responded. (Appendix B.)
- Data adjusted for geographic cost-of-labor (Economic Research Institute Geographic Assessor per prior analysis; confirm in Appendix).
- MGT recommends focusing on **salary** (benefits found broadly comparable).

## How this plugs into Article 2
1. **Fires + cost of living → higher percentile** was the county consultant's own logic — used for leadership pay (Fire Chief up to ~$239k, 75th-pct posture).
2. **No equivalent study exists for the firefighters.** Their pay went to arbitration and landed "lower than most contracts" (Chief, Apr 16) / sub-40th percentile with COLA (Mosher, May 21).
3. The union's cost-of-living argument isn't a talking point — **it's the same argument the county paid MGT to make for its directors.** That reframes the Chief's Jan 15 "the argument that the union uses" line devastatingly: the county's own consultant makes that identical argument, in writing, for management.
