<p class="lead-p">In March 2025, Maui County contracted MGT Consulting, a Tampa-based compensation firm, to conduct a Classification and Compensation Study for county Directors and Deputy Directors. The report, a 90-page draft, surveyed comparable jurisdictions across the western United States, analyzed salary ranges at the 50th through 80th percentiles, and recommended substantial pay increases for the county's top management tier.</p>

<p>The study cost the county money. It took months. And when you read it carefully, it makes a compelling case for raising firefighter pay, a case the county did not apply to its BU-11 firefighters, whose contract went to arbitration and came back below the field.</p>

<p>This is that case, made in the county's own words.</p>

<div class="divider">— ◆ —</div>

<h2 class="section-head">What the Study Was, and What It Wasn't</h2>

<p>Before going further, context matters. The MGT study covered Directors and Deputy Directors only, not frontline employees or bargaining-unit members. It was not a study of firefighter compensation and was not commissioned for that purpose. A full-report keyword search turns up no Firefighter, Captain, or Battalion Chief classifications anywhere in it. The only fire positions it prices are Fire Chief and Deputy Fire Chief.</p>

<p>But the reasoning MGT used to justify those raises, the logic, the data, the arguments, is not management-specific. Cost of living does not care what title is on your badge. A shrinking applicant pool affects entry-level hiring the same way it affects executive recruitment. Post-fire hardship was not experienced primarily by the people who managed the response from an office.</p>

<p>What follows is a direct reading of the study, with the obvious question asked at every step: if this argument justifies a raise for a Director, why does the same argument not justify a raise for a firefighter?</p>

<div class="divider">— ◆ —</div>

<h2 class="section-head">Argument One: Below-Market Pay</h2>

<div class="data-callout">
  <div class="callout-label">MGT's Argument for Directors</div>
  <p>The study's central finding was that Maui County's director salaries were below the 50th percentile of comparable jurisdictions. MGT surveyed comparable agencies and found that in category after category, Maui was paying its top managers less than what comparable organizations paid for equivalent roles.<br><br>For the Fire Chief specifically: at the time of the study, Maui County was paying <strong>$183,889</strong>. The 50th-percentile market range ran to <strong>$248,446</strong>, with comparable actual salaries averaging <strong>$232,368</strong>. Maui's Fire Chief was earning below what peer agencies paid for the same role.<br><br><em>MGT Draft Report, Appendix C, Fire Chief salary data.</em></p>
</div>

<div class="callout-dark">
  <span class="callout-label">The Same Argument Applied to BU-11 Firefighters, Stronger Here</span>
  <p>Hawaii firefighter wages have lagged comparable jurisdictions for years. The county's own Fire Chief told the Fire Commission in April 2026 that the firefighters' arbitration award "was lower than most of the contracts that have been negotiated and arbitrated this year." BU-11 firefighters received less than half the raise police received, and step increases were frozen for four years. The county's own consultant found it was paying below market for management. The same dynamic holds, on the record, for the people those managers supervise. (See <a href="/posts/hawaii_s_two-tier_public_safety_system">Hawaii's Two-Tier Public Safety System</a>.)</p>
</div>

<div class="divider">— ◆ —</div>

<h2 class="section-head">Argument Two: The Cost-of-Living Reality</h2>

<div class="data-callout">
  <div class="callout-label">MGT's Argument for Directors</div>
  <p>The study explicitly cited Maui's cost of living as a justification for paying at or above market. It stated that the cost of living in Maui County is <strong>1.3 times higher</strong> than the U.S. average, and that Hawaii ranks as the <strong>4th most expensive state</strong> to live in. MGT's conclusion: this cost-of-living premium makes it harder to attract and retain employees, and compensation decisions should account for it.<br><br><em>MGT Draft Report, p. 14.</em></p>
</div>

<div class="callout-dark">
  <span class="callout-label">The Same Argument Applied to BU-11 Firefighters</span>
  <p>A firefighter's rent, groceries, gas, and mortgage are purchased in the same Maui economy as a director's. The 1.3x cost-of-living premium does not apply at different rates based on job title. When a two-bedroom apartment in Kahului costs what it costs, it costs the same for a firefighter as it does for a department head. The cost-of-living argument MGT made for directors is not a management argument. It is a Maui argument, and it applies to everyone who lives here. The dollars-and-cents version for firefighters is in <a href="/posts/cost_of_living_gap_hawaii_firefighter_pay">The Cost-of-Living Gap</a>.</p>
</div>

<h2 class="section-head">Argument Three: Recruitment and Retention</h2>

<div class="data-callout">
  <div class="callout-label">MGT's Argument for Directors</div>
  <p>A central justification in the report was retention risk. The study argued that a properly developed compensation plan is essential if the county is to recruit the best employees and retain them in a competitive marketplace. It noted that losing qualified, experienced employees forces costly re-recruitments, results in lost productivity, and forfeits the institutional knowledge those employees have built over their careers.<br><br><em>MGT Draft Report, p. 2.</em></p>
</div>

<div class="callout-dark">
  <span class="callout-label">The Same Argument Applied to BU-11 Firefighters, Stronger Here</span>
  <p>MFD entrance-exam applicant pools have collapsed, and passing scores are dropping, documented signals that the pipeline of qualified candidates is narrowing. A firefighter cannot be onboarded in weeks the way an administrative hire sometimes can; the training pipeline alone takes years. An experienced Maui firefighter carries institutional knowledge that cannot be quickly reconstructed: familiarity with the island's roads, topography, communities, and water systems. The retention argument MGT made for management applies with greater urgency to the people who actually fight the fires. (See <a href="/posts/hawaii_firefighter_recruitment_collapse">The Line Out the Door Is Getting Shorter</a>.)</p>
</div>

<div class="divider">— ◆ —</div>

<h2 class="section-head">Argument Four: The August 8th Factor</h2>

<div class="data-callout">
  <div class="callout-label">MGT's Argument for Directors</div>
  <p>The MGT study included a specific acknowledgment that Directors and Deputy Directors of Maui County have faced numerous unique challenges in the aftermath of the tragic fires. It stated explicitly that this should be factored into the decision-making process when determining the appropriate pay percentile.<br><br><em>MGT Draft Report, p. 14.</em></p>
</div>

<div class="callout-dark">
  <span class="callout-label">The Same Argument Applied to BU-11 Firefighters, Significantly Stronger Here</span>
  <p>On August 8, 2023, Maui firefighters were not managing the Lahaina fire from a conference room. They were inside it. They pulled hose in hurricane-force winds. They made life-or-death decisions with insufficient water pressure and rapidly shifting fire behavior. They have lived with what they saw. The argument that post-fire hardship justifies higher compensation for the county's administrative leadership, while the men and women who physically fought the fire remained in an unresolved contract dispute, is a position that deserves public scrutiny. The study made the argument. The county chose who it applied to.</p>
</div>

<div class="pull-quote">
  <p>The county built the argument. Then decided it only applied to management.</p>
  <cite>Based on the MGT Draft Classification and Compensation Study, County of Maui (March 3, 2025)</cite>
</div>

<div class="divider">— ◆ —</div>

<h2 class="section-head">Argument Five: When Benefits Are Comparable, Salary Is the Gap</h2>

<div class="data-callout">
  <div class="callout-label">MGT's Argument for Directors</div>
  <p>MGT's benefit survey found that benefits offered across comparable public-sector entities are largely similar. The study concluded that because benefits are not a differentiator, the county should prioritize salary investment rather than changes to benefits. The implication: if you want to close the compensation gap, salary is where you close it.<br><br><em>MGT Draft Report, p. 11.</em></p>
</div>

<div class="callout-dark">
  <span class="callout-label">The Same Argument Applied to BU-11 Firefighters</span>
  <p>The same finding holds for BU-11. Firefighter benefits across Hawaii counties are broadly comparable to peer departments. The compensation gap is a salary and overtime gap, compounded by the FLSA Section 7(k) exemption, a federal provision Maui County voluntarily chooses to apply. That exemption costs a Maui firefighter an estimated <strong>$475,000</strong> in foregone overtime over a 25-year career at current wage rates. The full breakdown is in <a href="/posts/the_federal_exemption_that_costs_hawaii_firefighters_millions">The Federal Exemption That Costs Hawaii Firefighters Millions</a>. The case MGT made for closing the salary gap at the management level applies to the salary and overtime gap at the firefighter level.</p>
</div>

<div class="divider">— ◆ —</div>

<h2 class="section-head">What the County Actually Did</h2>

<p>This is where the study stops being a hypothetical. The county did not just receive MGT's logic. It acted on it, for management, twice, while the firefighters' contract went the other way. The record reads as a split screen.</p>

<div class="data-callout">
  <div class="callout-label">The Management Track</div>
  <p>The Fire Chief's salary, <strong>$183,889</strong> at the time of the study, was raised to roughly <strong>$239,043</strong> in April 2025, an increase of about 30 percent. In a May 8, 2026 letter to the Salary Commission, the Fire and Public Safety Commission then reasserted a <strong>75th-percentile</strong> posture for the Fire Chief and Deputy (about <strong>$258,930</strong> and <strong>$233,037</strong>), citing the MGT study, a 23.3 percent cost-of-living adjustment, and post-wildfire duties. The county's own FY2026 budget carries the raise as a named line item: "Council transferred increases for Fire Chief and Deputy Fire Chief per Salary Commission," about <strong>$208,011</strong>.</p>
</div>

<div class="callout-dark">
  <span class="callout-label">The Firefighter Track</span>
  <p>Over the same window, the BU-11 firefighters' contract went to binding arbitration and came back with across-the-board raises of 3, 3, 2.5, and 2.5 percent over four years and <strong>no step advancement</strong>, an outcome the Fire Chief himself described to the Commission as "lower than most" contracts arbitrated that year. Adjusted for cost of living, firefighter pay sits below the 40th percentile nationally. What the Commission was told about that gap, and what the records show, is its own story: <a href="/posts/maui_fire_commission_was_told_firefighters_do_fairly_well">Maui's Fire Commission Was Told Firefighters Do "Fairly Well."</a></p>
</div>

<p>Same study. Same cost of living. Same wildfire. The county applied the 75th-percentile, cost-of-living, post-fire logic to its department heads and left the firefighters to arbitration. That is the whole point.</p>

<div class="divider">— ◆ —</div>

<h2 class="section-head">What the Community Should Take From This</h2>

<p>This article is not an argument about whether county directors deserve competitive pay. They do. The MGT study makes a reasonable case, and compensating county leadership competitively serves the public interest.</p>

<p>This article is about consistency, and about what it means when a government entity commissions a professional study, accepts its findings for one group of employees, and declines to apply the same logic to another. The county paid for the argument. The argument is sound. It just was not applied to the people fighting the fires.</p>

<div class="callout-dark">
  <span class="callout-label">Plain English</span>
  <p>Maui County hired consultants who said: you are paying below market, your cost of living is 1.3 times the national average, you need to compete for talent, and the fires created hardship that should be reflected in compensation. The county agreed and raised its Fire Chief's pay about 30 percent, toward a 75th-percentile target. BU-11 firefighters, who live in the same Maui economy, face the same recruitment pressures, and physically fought the Lahaina fire, got an arbitration award their own Chief called lower than most.</p>
</div>

<ul class="article-list">
  <li>Every core argument MGT used to justify director pay increases applies equally to BU-11 firefighters, in several cases with greater force.</li>
  <li>Maui's cost of living at 1.3 times the national average affects firefighters and directors identically. It is a Maui argument, not a management argument.</li>
  <li>MFD applicant-pool data shows a shrinking, lower-scoring candidate pipeline, making the retention argument more urgent for frontline positions, not less.</li>
  <li>The MGT study specifically cited post-fire hardship as a pay justification. That argument is stronger for the people who physically fought the fire than for those who administered the response.</li>
  <li>The county acted on the study for management (a roughly 30 percent Fire Chief raise, a 75th-percentile target) while the firefighters' contract went to arbitration and came back below the field.</li>
  <li>The county's own consultants built the case for competitive pay. The question is who the county decides that case applies to.</li>
</ul>

<p>The MGT Classification and Compensation Study was commissioned by Maui County and submitted in draft form in March 2025. If you believe the county's own reasoning should apply to the firefighters who serve the same communities, make that known to your county council member, your state legislator, or your mayor's office.</p>
