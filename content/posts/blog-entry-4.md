<p class="lead-p">In March 2025, Maui County contracted MGT Consulting — a Tampa-based compensation firm — to conduct a Classification and Compensation Study for county Directors and Deputy Directors. The report, a 90-page draft, surveyed comparable jurisdictions across the western United States, analyzed salary ranges at the 50th through 80th percentiles, and recommended substantial pay increases for the county's top management tier.</p>

<p>The study cost the county money. It took months. And when you read it carefully, it makes a compelling case for raising firefighter pay — a case the county has not applied to its BU-11 firefighters in active contract negotiations.</p>

<p>This is that case, made in the county's own words.</p>

<div class="divider">— ◆ —</div>

<h2 class="section-head">What the Study Was — and What It Wasn't</h2>

<p>Before going further, context matters. The MGT study covered Directors and Deputy Directors only — not frontline employees or bargaining unit members. It was not a study of firefighter compensation and was not commissioned for that purpose.</p>

<p>But the reasoning MGT used to justify those raises — the logic, the data, the arguments — is not management-specific. Cost of living doesn't care what title is on your badge. A shrinking applicant pool affects entry-level hiring the same way it affects executive recruitment. Post-fire hardship was not experienced primarily by the people who managed the response from an office.</p>

<p>What follows is a direct reading of the study — with the obvious question asked at every step: if this argument justifies a raise for a Director, why doesn't the same argument justify a raise for a firefighter?</p>

<div class="divider">— ◆ —</div>

<h2 class="section-head">Argument One: Below-Market Pay</h2>

<div class="data-callout">
  <div class="callout-label">MGT's Argument for Directors</div>
  <p>The study's central finding was that Maui County's director salaries were below the 50th percentile of comparable jurisdictions. MGT surveyed 26 agencies and found that in category after category, Maui was paying its top managers less than what comparable organizations paid for equivalent roles.<br><br>For the Fire Chief specifically: Maui County was paying <strong>$183,889</strong>. The 50th-percentile market range showed <strong>$179,523–$248,446</strong>, with comparable actual salaries averaging <strong>$232,368</strong>. Maui's Fire Chief was earning well below what peer agencies paid for the same role.<br><br><em>MGT Draft Report, Appendix C — Fire Chief salary data, p. 62</em></p>
</div>

<div class="callout-dark">
  <span class="callout-label">The Same Argument Applied to BU-11 Firefighters — Stronger Here</span>
  <p>Hawaii firefighter wages have lagged comparable jurisdictions for years. The HFFA's own market analysis — and the county's willingness to settle with SHOPO for a substantially higher raise for BU-12 police — confirms the gap. BU-11 firefighters received less than half the raise police received, and step increases were frozen for four years. The county's own consultant found it was paying below market for management; the same dynamic holds for the people those managers supervise.</p>
</div>

<div class="divider">— ◆ —</div>

<h2 class="section-head">Argument Two: The Cost-of-Living Reality</h2>

<div class="data-callout">
  <div class="callout-label">MGT's Argument for Directors</div>
  <p>The study explicitly cited Maui's cost of living as a justification for paying at or above market. It stated that the cost of living in Maui County is <strong>1.3 times higher</strong> than the U.S. average, and noted that Hawaii ranks as the <strong>4th most expensive state</strong> to live in. MGT's conclusion: this cost-of-living premium makes it harder to attract and retain employees, and compensation decisions should account for it.<br><br><em>MGT Draft Report, p. 14</em></p>
</div>

<div class="callout-dark">
  <span class="callout-label">The Same Argument Applied to BU-11 Firefighters</span>
  <p>A firefighter's rent, groceries, gas, and mortgage are purchased in the same Maui economy as a director's. The 1.3× cost-of-living premium does not apply at different rates based on job title. When a two-bedroom apartment in Kahului costs what it costs, it costs the same for a firefighter as it does for a department head. The cost-of-living argument MGT made for directors is not a management argument. It is a Maui argument — and it applies to everyone who lives here.</p>
</div>

<h2 class="section-head">Argument Three: Recruitment and Retention</h2>

<div class="data-callout">
  <div class="callout-label">MGT's Argument for Directors</div>
  <p>A central justification in the report was retention risk. The study argued that a properly developed compensation plan is essential if the county is to recruit the best employees and retain them in a competitive marketplace. It noted that losing qualified, experienced employees forces costly re-recruitments, results in lost productivity, and forfeits the institutional knowledge those employees have built over their careers.<br><br><em>MGT Draft Report, p. 2</em></p>
</div>

<div class="callout-dark">
  <span class="callout-label">The Same Argument Applied to BU-11 Firefighters — Stronger Here</span>
  <p>MFD written-exam applicant pools have been shrinking, and passing scores are dropping — documented signals that the pipeline of qualified candidates is narrowing. A firefighter cannot be onboarded in weeks the way an administrative hire sometimes can; the training pipeline alone takes years. An experienced Maui firefighter carries institutional knowledge that cannot be quickly reconstructed: familiarity with the island's roads, topography, communities, and water systems. The retention argument MGT made for management applies with greater urgency to the people who actually fight the fires.</p>
</div>

<div class="divider">— ◆ —</div>

<h2 class="section-head">Argument Four: The August 8th Factor</h2>

<div class="data-callout">
  <div class="callout-label">MGT's Argument for Directors</div>
  <p>The MGT study included a specific acknowledgment that directors and deputy directors of Maui County have faced numerous unique challenges in the aftermath of the tragic fires. It stated explicitly that this should be factored into the decision-making process when determining the appropriate pay percentile.<br><br><em>MGT Draft Report, p. 14</em></p>
</div>

<div class="callout-dark">
  <span class="callout-label">The Same Argument Applied to BU-11 Firefighters — Significantly Stronger Here</span>
  <p>On August 8, 2023, Maui firefighters were not managing the Lahaina fire from a conference room. They were inside it. They pulled hose in hurricane-force winds. They made life-or-death decisions with insufficient water pressure and rapidly shifting fire behavior. They have lived with what they saw. The argument that post-fire hardship justifies higher compensation for the county's administrative leadership — while the men and women who physically fought the fire remain in an unresolved contract dispute — is a position that deserves public scrutiny. The study made the argument. The county chose who it applied to.</p>
</div>

<div class="pull-quote">
  <p>The county built the argument. Then decided it only applied to management.</p>
  <cite>— Based on the MGT Draft Classification and Compensation Study, County of Maui (March 3, 2025)</cite>
</div>

<div class="divider">— ◆ —</div>

<h2 class="section-head">Argument Five: When Benefits Are Comparable, Salary Is the Gap</h2>

<div class="data-callout">
  <div class="callout-label">MGT's Argument for Directors</div>
  <p>MGT's benefit survey found that benefits offered across comparable public-sector entities are largely similar. The study concluded that because benefits are not a differentiator, the county should prioritize salary investment rather than changes to benefits. The implication: if you want to close the compensation gap, salary is where you close it.<br><br><em>MGT Draft Report, p. 11</em></p>
</div>

<div class="callout-dark">
  <span class="callout-label">The Same Argument Applied to BU-11 Firefighters</span>
  <p>The same finding holds for BU-11. Firefighter benefits across Hawaii counties are broadly comparable to peer departments. The compensation gap is a salary and overtime gap — compounded by the FLSA Section 7(k) exemption, a federal provision Maui County voluntarily chooses to apply. That exemption costs Maui firefighters on the confirmed Kelly schedule roughly $1,463 in foregone overtime every 28-day cycle — an estimated $475,000 over a 25-year career at current wage rates. The case MGT made for closing the salary gap at the management level applies to the salary and overtime gap at the firefighter level.</p>
</div>

<div class="divider">— ◆ —</div>

<h2 class="section-head">The Numbers, Side by Side</h2>

<p>To make this concrete: the MGT study recommended that the Maui County Fire Chief's salary move toward a proposed range of <strong>$165,000–$222,750</strong> at the 50th percentile — already above the $183,889 being paid. The Deputy Fire Chief was similarly flagged as below market. Those recommendations rest on the premise that Maui County must pay competitively to recruit and retain qualified public safety leadership in a high-cost-of-living environment where comparable jurisdictions pay more. That premise does not change when the rank changes.</p>

<h2 class="section-head">What the Community Should Take From This</h2>

<p>This article is not an argument about whether county directors deserve competitive pay. They do. The MGT study makes a reasonable case, and compensating county leadership competitively serves the public interest.</p>

<p>This article is about consistency — and about what it means when a government entity commissions a professional study, accepts its findings for one group of employees, and declines to apply the same logic to another. The county paid for the argument. The argument is sound. It just wasn't applied to the people fighting the fires.</p>

<div class="callout-dark">
  <span class="callout-label">Plain English</span>
  <p>Maui County hired consultants who said: you're paying below market, your cost of living is 1.3× the national average, you need to compete for talent, and the fires created hardship that should be reflected in compensation. The county said: agreed — and gave raises to directors. BU-11 firefighters, who live in the same Maui economy, face the same recruitment pressures, and physically fought the Lahaina fire, remain in an unresolved contract dispute.</p>
</div>

<ul class="article-list">
  <li>Every core argument MGT used to justify director pay increases applies equally to BU-11 firefighters — in several cases with greater force.</li>
  <li>Maui's cost of living at 1.3× the national average affects firefighters and directors identically. It is a Maui argument, not a management argument.</li>
  <li>MFD applicant-pool data shows a shrinking, lower-scoring candidate pipeline — making the retention argument more urgent for frontline positions, not less.</li>
  <li>The MGT study specifically cited post-fire hardship as a pay justification. That argument is stronger for the people who physically fought the fire than for those who administered the response.</li>
  <li>Maui firefighters lose an estimated $14,000–$21,000 per year in foregone overtime under a federal exemption the county voluntarily applies.</li>
  <li>The county's own consultants built the case for competitive pay. The question is who the county decides that case applies to.</li>
</ul>

<p>The MGT Classification and Compensation Study was commissioned by Maui County and submitted in draft form in March 2025. If you believe the county's own reasoning should apply to the firefighters who serve the same communities, make that known to your county council member, your state legislator, or your mayor's office.</p>
